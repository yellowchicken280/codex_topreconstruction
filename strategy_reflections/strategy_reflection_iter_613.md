# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 613 Report

The 0.616 ± 0.0152 efficiency shows that the physics‑driven ingredients of strategy_v651 helped to recover many genuine tops – the pT‑dependent Gaussian for the top mass and the logistic pT term both lifted the high‑pT tail – yet the combination fell short of the 0.64 goal because the penalties and weightings did not sufficiently suppress the combinatorial background and the aggressive two‑stage veto trimmed away a non‑negligible fraction of correctly‑reconstructed tops; in particular, the asymmetry factor w_asym was applied with a modest exponent, the non‑linear product constrained the sum of exponents to unity which limited the freedom to favour the most discriminating terms, and the 0.95‑power softening of the raw BDT, while reducing outliers, also diluted the genuine BDT‑driven separation.  To push the efficiency higher we should re‑optimise the hyper‑parameters (e.g. increase the slope of σ_top from 0.02 GeV/GeV to ≃0.03 GeV/GeV, raise the asymmetry exponent or introduce a separate asymmetry‑penalty term), relax the strict sum‑to‑one constraint (allowing a small “bias” term or using a log‑sum‑exp combiner), and revisit the logistic pT centre (perhaps moving it to 150 GeV or making it pT‑dependent itself).  Additionally, incorporating complementary discriminants such as b‑tag scores, ΔR_{jj} of the W‑candidate pair, and a χ²‑like compatibility of all three dijet masses could provide a more powerful multivariate penalty for pathological triplets.  Finally, rather than a hard two‑stage veto, a soft, data‑driven “probability of failure” weighting (or a small boosted‑decision‑tree trained on the veto variables) would preserve the high‑quality signal while still rejecting clear background.  A systematic grid‑search or Bayesian optimisation of these revised weights and thresholds, validated on an independent control sample, should be the next step toward reliably crossing the 0.64 efficiency barrier.
