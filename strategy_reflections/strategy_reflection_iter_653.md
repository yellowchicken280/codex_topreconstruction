# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 653 Report

The modest 0.616 ± 0.015 efficiency indicates that the physics‑driven refinements of strategy_v653 did not translate into the hoped‑for gain—most likely because the added smoothings and penalties over‑damped the genuine top‑signal features rather than amplifying them: the Gaussian mass windows, while well‑intentioned, blurred the sharp W‑mass peak and weakened the discriminator’s ability to reject combinatorial background; the logistic boost factor and tanh‑scaled BDT softened the high‑pT and extreme‑score tails, unintentionally admitting more background; the asymmetry term, though effective at discarding wildly unbalanced dijet pairings, also trimmed legitimate asymmetric decays; and the interaction terms introduced extra non‑linearities that, without careful regularisation, appear to have diluted rather than synergised the BDT information. Moreover, the early two‑stage veto may have been too aggressive, cutting away a non‑negligible fraction of recoverable signal. To move forward, a systematic hyper‑parameter optimisation (e.g. Bayesian scan of Gaussian widths, logistic steepness, tanh scaling, and asymmetry weighting) should be performed, possibly with per‑event mass‑resolution inputs from kinematic fits to restore the sharpness of the mass windows; the interaction term could be re‑implemented as a calibrated multiplicative factor rather than a raw product to avoid over‑smoothing; and an alternative non‑linear combiner (e.g. a shallow neural network or a boosted decision tree that ingests the physics‑derived features) could be trained to learn the optimal synergy between BDT scores and mass‑based weights. Finally, re‑evaluating the early veto thresholds to retain borderline signal events while still eliminating clearly background‑like candidates will help ensure that the refined scoring actually lifts events just below the acceptance cutoff, bringing the efficiency past the 0.64 barrier.
