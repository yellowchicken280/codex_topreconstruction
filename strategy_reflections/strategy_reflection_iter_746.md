# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 746 Report

The modest but notable gain to 61.6 % ± 1.5 % efficiency shows that the p_T‑dependent Gaussian pulls and the softened mass‑term exponents succeeded in rescuing genuinely smeared top candidates that would have been rejected by a rigid ±15 GeV window, while the sigmoid p_T factor and tanh‑scaled BDT kept low‑p_T objects from being completely discarded; however, the same liberalizations also diluted the discriminating power of the mass and asymmetry observables, allowing more background to bleed into the high‑score region and keeping us short of the >65 % target. In particular, the floor of 0.02 on the pulls may be too permissive for low‑p_T jets, the broad Gaussian asymmetry penalty (centered at the expected 1.5 dijet‑mass ratio) is insufficiently punitive for radiation‑induced imbalances, and the tanh smoothing (β < 1) overly flattens the BDT tail. Going forward we should (i) re‑tune the σ_top(p_T) and σ_W(p_T) functions using a data‑driven resolution curve to better match the true detector smearing, (ii) tighten the asymmetry Gaussian width or make it p_T‑dependent, (iii) explore a steeper sigmoidal p_T weighting (e.g. adjust the midpoint and slope) so that low‑p_T candidates are down‑weighted without being lost, (iv) experiment with β values closer to 1 or replace the tanh transform with a calibrated logistic scaling that preserves tail separation, and (v) introduce a secondary multivariate classifier that ingests the softened scores plus additional sub‑structure variables (e.g. jet‑mass drop, N‑subjettiness) to recover background rejection while preserving the newfound signal acceptance. A systematic scan of these hyper‑parameters on both signal‑only and mixed validation samples, followed by a fresh efficiency‑vs‑background‑rejection curve, will tell whether these refinements can push the reconstruction efficiency past the 65 % threshold without compromising purity.
