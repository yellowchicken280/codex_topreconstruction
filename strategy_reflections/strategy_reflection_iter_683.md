# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 683 Report

The modest gain to 61.6 % efficiency shows that the pT‑dependent resolution scaling and the product‑based factor combination succeeded in loosening the overly tight Gaussian pulls that were killing low‑pT tops and in rewarding candidates that simultaneously satisfy all calibrated criteria, while the soft‑max over the three dijet masses and the tanh‑shaped pT uplift helped preserve the most “W‑like’’ and well‑measured triplets; however, the multiplicative scheme still proves too unforgiving—any single factor slipping below unity drags the whole score down, which in practice removes a non‑negligible fraction of genuine tops with one marginal sub‑observable (e.g. a slightly broadened dijet spread or a modest b‑tag weight), and the hard veto on large dijet spreads can excise legitimate high‑mass tails arising from detector effects. The next iteration should therefore focus on softening the harshness of the product by moving to a log‑sum‑exp or weighted‑additive combination that tolerates one weak factor, retuning the σ (pT) scaling and tanh‑uplift parameters (perhaps via a data‑driven resolution model) and introducing a temperature parameter for the soft‑max to control how strongly the best W‑candidate dominates. Additionally, a more nuanced spread penalty—e.g. a continuous penalty function modulated by the raw BDT score—could replace the binary veto, and the inclusion of extra discriminants (b‑tag likelihoods, N‑subjettiness, energy‑correlation functions) into a second‑stage multivariate could recover events that the current score suppresses. Systematic cross‑validation on independent samples and a focused ROC‑curve study will then reveal whether these refinements boost efficiency without sacrificing background rejection.
