# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 703 Report

The 0.616 ± 0.015 efficiency achieved with strategy_topv2 falls short of the desired >0.64 ceiling, indicating that the smoothing and “soft‑veto” ideas helped to some extent (the baseline tagger was hovering near 0.58) but also introduced new sources of inefficiency: the geometric‑mean of three W‑mass Gaussians successfully tolerates a single outlier but simultaneously dilutes the discriminating power of a sharply peaked W‑mass hypothesis, especially when the three probabilities are all modest; the pT‑dependent resolution model widens the top‑ and W‑mass windows enough to rescue a few ultra‑high‑pT candidates, yet the widening is probably too aggressive, allowing more background‑like jet‐mass fluctuations to survive; the tanh non‑linearity and multiplicative combination remove the binary‑veto effect but compress the dynamic range of the raw BDT score and force low‑score components to dominate the product, effectively penalising otherwise good tops that happen to have a slightly weaker sub‑component; finally, the logarithmic pT weight, while gently boosting the highest‑pT regime, may over‑emphasise regions where the background tails are large, eroding the overall signal‑to‑background gain. To move beyond the current plateau the next iteration should (i) re‑tune the exponents and the tanh scaling (perhaps replacing it with a calibrated monotonic spline that preserves more of the BDT’s discrimination), (ii) test a pT‑bin‑dependent set of Gaussian widths rather than a single smooth function, (iii) explore a hybrid approach where the geometric‑mean is applied only for candidates that already pass a modest W‑mass likelihood threshold, and (iv) incorporate additional decorrelated substructure variables (e.g. N‑subjettiness ratios, groomed masses, b‑tag discriminants) into a second‑stage classifier that can recover efficiency lost by the softened mass cuts while keeping background rejection under control. Systematic cross‑validation on independent MC samples and a data‑driven closure test will be essential to confirm that any gain is robust and not an artifact of over‑smoothing.
