# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 652 Report

The modest but statistically significant gain to an overall efficiency of 0.616 ± 0.015 suggests that the soft‑kernel, Gaussian‑likelihood, and mild‑sigmoid refinements successfully rescued a sizeable fraction of signal candidates that the previous hard‑veto implementation would have thrown away, especially in the intermediate‑boost regime where traditional cuts lose power; by preserving the full dynamic range of the BDT and rewarding mildly‑boosted triplets, the new score remains sensitive to the subtle correlations that a steep sigmoid would compress, while the Gaussian treatment of the W‑ and top‑mass windows naturally incorporates detector resolution and avoids binary penalties for near‑on‑shell events. Nonetheless, the linear weighted sum may be under‑exploiting the non‑linear interplay between the BDT output and the physics‑based terms—potentially double‑counting information or suppressing useful tail events—and the current weight choices (strongest emphasis on the W‑mass term) and kernel widths were set heuristically, leaving room for sub‑optimal trade‑offs between signal efficiency and background rejection. The next step should therefore be a systematic hyper‑parameter scan (or Bayesian optimisation) of the Gaussian widths, sigmoid slope, p_T‑weight shape, and linear coefficients, possibly replacing the linear combination with a shallow neural‑network or boosted‑tree meta‑learner to capture higher‑order interactions, while also re‑evaluating the early‑reject threshold to ensure it does not excise atypical but genuine signal topologies; cross‑validation on independent data splits and monitoring of ROC‑AUC / SIC curves will clarify whether these refinements can push the efficiency well above the current 62 % without inflating the background rate.
