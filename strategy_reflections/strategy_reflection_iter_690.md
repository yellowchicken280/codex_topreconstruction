# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 690 Report

The new strategy lifted the efficiency to 0.616 ± 0.0152 mainly because the hard, punitive χ²‑based cuts were replaced with smooth, physics‑motivated functions: the logistic‑scaled BDT kept its discriminating power without over‑penalising borderline scores, the sigmoid pT weight rescued the crucial 300–500 GeV regime where the three‑jet system naturally spreads, Gaussian factors centred on the world‑average top and W masses correctly modeled detector resolution, and a variance‑based spread penalty softened the previous quadratic mass‑balance term while still discouraging pathological W‑mass configurations; together with a generous hard veto (130–210 GeV top window, ±30 GeV W tolerance) they retained more genuine tops while curbing combinatorial background. The remaining shortfall from the 65 % target and the modest statistical uncertainty suggest that the hard veto is still discarding a non‑negligible fraction of signal—particularly events with slightly shifted reconstructed masses due to jet‑energy scale variations, gluon radiation, or resolution tails—and that the spread‑penalty strength may be overly aggressive for moderate‑boost tops. The next step is to iteratively retune these hyper‑parameters (e.g. widen the top‑mass window, adjust the Gaussian σ to match in‑situ jet resolution, and calibrate the variance‑penalty coefficient) while monitoring background contamination; a systematic study of the mis‑tagged events will reveal whether an additional discriminant such as b‑tag multiplicity or subjet substructure should be introduced, and cross‑validation on independent MC samples and early data will ensure robustness before finalising the working point.
