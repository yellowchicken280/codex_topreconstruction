# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 647 Report

The jump to an efficiency of 0.616 ± 0.015 shows that the new recipe succeeded at loosening the overly‑tight Gaussian window while still curbing combinatorial background – early vetoes removed the most egregious jet triplets, asymmetric Gaussian likelihoods gave the W‑ and top‑mass terms realistic tails that absorb detector smearing, the sigmoid boost rescued low‑pT tops that were previously under‑represented, the tanh‑scaled BDT kept the multivariate score from dominating when poorly calibrated, and the spread‑penalty plus tight‑window rescue factor added sensible physics‑driven regularisation; together, the multiplicative, exponent‑weighted combination re‑balanced the contributions so that modestly plausible candidates could still achieve a high score. Nonetheless, the multiplicative form still harshly penalises any single out‑of‑range term, which likely caps the gain and may degrade purity, and the exponents and functional shapes were tuned by hand rather than systematically, leaving room for sub‑optimal weighting across pₜ regimes. The next step should therefore be a rigorous hyper‑parameter optimisation (e.g. Bayesian or grid search) of the Gaussian asymmetries, sigmoid steepness, tanh scaling, and exponent values, complemented by cross‑validation on independent simulated samples and data control regions; alternative combination schemes such as a log‑sum‑exp or a shallow neural‑net aggregator could be benchmarked to soften the multiplicative suppression, and the rescue window could be made pₜ‑dependent to avoid over‑boosting low‑pₜ background. Finally, adding an explicit b‑tag weight and validating the full chain on a blinded data set will reveal whether the observed efficiency translates into a genuine increase in signal significance and robustness against systematic uncertainties.
