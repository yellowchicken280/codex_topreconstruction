# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 608 Report

The modest jump to 0.616 ± 0.015—still shy of the 0.65 goal—suggests that the physics‑driven tweaks are moving the right way but are not yet fully exploiting the available discrimination: the dynamic resolution model correctly widens the mass sigma for low‑pₜ tops, rescuing some otherwise‑lost candidates, yet its √(1 + pₜ^{ref}/pₜ) scaling may be too aggressive and lets in additional combinatorial background; the soft two‑out‑of‑three pairing weight recovers efficiency lost by the prior hard veto, but retaining only the product of the two most compatible Gaussian probabilities still penalises events where both W‑mass hypotheses are mildly shifted, limiting gains; the tanh‑based BDT mapping does preserve the high‑score tail but sacrifices granularity in the bulk region where most signal–background separation occurs; the gentle pₜ uplift smooths the threshold region but its 250 GeV scale may be suboptimal for the spectrum at hand; finally, the fixed exponents in the weighted geometric mean likely under‑weight the pairing term relative to the BDT, preventing the score from capitalising on partial successes. To push beyond the current ceiling, a systematic hyper‑parameter optimisation—varying pₜ^{ref}, the uplift scale, the exponents, and the tanh‑stretch factor—combined with a data‑driven validation of the resolution model (e.g. using side‑band dijet masses) is essential, and it may be worthwhile to replace the handcrafted multiplicative aggregation with a shallow neural‑network or boosted‑tree meta‑combiner that can learn optimal non‑linear weightings; incorporating additional discriminants such as subjet‑level b‑tag scores or N‑subjettiness, and cross‑checking performance on independent MC and early data control regions, will further clarify whether the current bottleneck is model bias, residual mis‑measurement, or an over‑constrained combination scheme.
