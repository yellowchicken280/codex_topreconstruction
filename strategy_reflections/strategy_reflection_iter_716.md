# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 716 Report

The modest rise to ε≈0.616 ± 0.0152 shows that the new ingredients – a piecewise‑linear σ that tightens the Gaussian likelihood at very large pT, a softened BDT exponent (0.85) that redistributes weight toward moderate‑score triplets, the smooth asymmetry penalty 1/(1+(asym‑1)^6) that screens out pathological dijet‑mass spreads, the sigmoid pT envelope centred at 200 GeV and the hard veto on triplets with all three dijet masses > 35 GeV from m_W – indeed rescued a fraction of the “missing ~1 %” efficiency that the previous logarithmic σ and harsher cuts were discarding. In practice, the mass‑resolution gain for boosted tops and the gentler boost‑weighting together allowed more borderline candidates to survive the likelihood, while the asymmetry factor preserved the purity of genuine three‑body top decays; however, the residual statistical uncertainty and the fact that the overall gain is only ∼2 % suggest that further refinements are still needed. The asymmetry exponent (sixth power) may be over‑suppressing near‑balanced but slightly skewed configurations, and the fixed 35 GeV mass‑window veto does not account for the pT‑dependent broadening of the W‑mass peak, potentially discarding recoverable high‑pT tops. Going forward, one could (i) replace the hard veto with a pT‑scaled dynamic window, (ii) optimise the sigmoid width (≈45 GeV) or centre it by profiling on data, (iii) fine‑tune the BDT exponent (e.g. explore 0.75–0.9) with a cross‑validated grid search, (iv) experiment with a smoother asymmetry penalty (e.g. a logistic rather than a high‑order polynomial) to retain mildly asymmetric triplets, and (v) augment the score with substructure observables (τ_32, ECFs) or a shallow neural‑network combiner to capture residual correlations. These targeted adjustments should push the efficiency closer to the ~0.63 goal while keeping the interpretability and robustness of the physics‑motivated likelihood framework.
