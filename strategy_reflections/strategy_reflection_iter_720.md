# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 720 Report

The modest rise to 0.616 ± 0.015 in signal efficiency shows that letting the detector‑driven mass resolution shrink with increasing top pₜ and replacing the crude geometric mean with a log‑sum‑exp for the three dijet masses does improve the discrimination power—especially by allowing the best‑matching W candidate to dominate while still penalising badly‑matched pairs—but the gain was insufficient to breach the 64 % target because the Gaussian‑shaped asymmetry penalty is still too gentle, the tanh‑scaled pₜ boost saturates before fully exploiting the extra kinematic leverage of very energetic tops, and the hard veto, although selective, still discards a non‑trivial fraction of borderline signal events that would have been rescued by a smoother penalty. To move beyond the barrier we should (1) re‑tune the width of the Gaussian asymmetry penalty and possibly make it pₜ‑dependent so that high‑pₜ tops are less harshly penalised, (2) experiment with a steeper but still bounded pₜ‑weighting function (e.g. a shifted arctan or a piecewise linear boost) to give stronger emphasis to the highest‑energy tops without runaway scores, (3) soften or replace the binary veto with a continuous “mass‑pull” term that can be incorporated into the LSE combination, and (4) augment the score with additional substructure observables (e.g. N‑subjettiness or energy‑correlation ratios) and re‑optimise the full set of hyper‑parameters on an independent validation sample. A systematic hyper‑parameter scan and an ROC‑curve‑driven selection of the optimal operating point should reveal whether these refinements can push the efficiency past the 0.64 threshold while preserving background rejection.
