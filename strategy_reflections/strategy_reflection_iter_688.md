# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 688 Report

The modest gain to 0.616 ± 0.015 (still ~2 % below the 64 % goal) tells us that the physics‑driven tweaks largely succeeded in softening the hard cuts that were killing good tops – the milder BDT exponent preserved high‑scoring candidates, the sigmoid‑plus‑quadratic pT weight rescued midspectrum tops, and the pT‑dependent Gaussian mass windows prevented over‑pruning at high boost – but the remaining shortfall points to the new ingredients being either still too aggressive (the quadratic boost may be rewarding only the most extreme pT values, leaving a gap around 300–500 GeV) or insufficiently discriminating (the linear growth of σ with pT could be admitting too much combinatorial background, and the mass‑balance penalty based on the relative RMS of the three dijet masses may still be penalising realistic top decays with modest mass spread).  The next step is to fine‑tune these functional forms: loosen the quadratic term (e.g. reduce its coefficient or replace it with a softer power‑law), explore a broader sigmoid transition (adjust the midpoint and steepness) to better cover the midspectrum, and introduce a calibrated scaling factor for the mass‑balance term so that typical top‑decay spreads are not over‑penalised.  In parallel we should test adding complementary information – such as b‑tagging scores or jet‑substructure observables (τ₃/τ₂, energy‑correlation functions) – either as multiplicative modifiers or as a final shallow BDT that learns the optimal combination of the analytically‑derived scores.  A small grid search over the exponent (0.6–0.8), σ‑growth rates (0.02–0.06 GeV per GeV pT), and mass‑balance weighting will let us quantify the sensitivity of the efficiency to each tweak and, with the current statistical precision, should push us past the 0.64 threshold.
