# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 670 Report

The modest gain from strategy_v670 shows that loosening the Gaussian mass windows, using pT‑dependent σ’s and Student‑t kernels, and allowing all dijet‑mass hypotheses to contribute did indeed rescue a few genuine top candidates that were previously thrown away in the resolution tails, but the price of the extra flexibility was a simultaneous rise in background‐like combinations that diluted the discriminating power of the downstream BDT and the final geometric‑mean score, leaving the overall reconstruction efficiency at 0.616 ± 0.015—still short of the 0.64 target; in hindsight the overly soft averaging of the three dijet‑mass likelihoods, the aggressive pT‑dependent sigmoid prior (which over‑favours the modal pT region) and the fixed α correlation term appear to have introduced too much “noise” while the logistic mapping of the raw BDT output compressed the high‑score tail that carries most of the signal information. The next iteration should therefore focus on re‑balancing flexibility and selectivity: (i) re‑tune the Student‑t ν and the pT‑dependence of σ (perhaps using a data‑driven resolution model) to retain tail efficiency without inflating the background, (ii) replace the simple soft‑average with a weighted likelihood that down‑weights low‑mass‑compatible W candidates, (iii) calibrate the BDT output with isotonic regression rather than a fixed logistic to preserve tail separation, (iv) explore a more forgiving combination scheme—e.g. a shallow neural‑net or boosted‑averaging that learns optimal component weights and a tunable α rather than a static power—while (v) performing a systematic hyper‑parameter scan (σ(pT) slopes, ν, α, sigmoid shape, geometric‑mean exponents) using cross‑validation on both simulated and control‑region data. By iterating these refinements and validating each change on an independent validation set, we can aim to push the efficiency above the 0.64 barrier without sacrificing background rejection.
