# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 740 Report

The modest gain to 61.6 % shows that the pT‑dependent sigmoid and the Gaussian triplet‑mass likelihood indeed softened the hard χ² wall and rescued a few low‑pT tops that the legacy cut would have discarded, but the two‑stage veto and the tanh asymmetry penalty still removed too many borderline candidates, especially when the W‑mass window was tightened to guard against pathological dijets; in practice the σW scaling proved insufficiently aggressive at the lowest pT, leaving a residual bias that the soft score threshold could not overcome, and the fixed width of the Gaussian likelihood was still narrower than the true resolution tail, causing an over‑penalisation of legitimately smeared tops. To push past the 64 % goal we should first re‑tune the core hyper‑parameters: broaden the mass‑likelihood σ as a function of jet‑energy resolution, relax the hard dijet‑mass windows (or replace them with a smooth pT‑scaled penalty), and experiment with a softer asymmetry function (e.g. a logistic rather than tanh) that retains mildly unbalanced triplets. A systematic grid‑search or Bayesian optimisation on an independent validation set would quantify the trade‑off between efficiency and background leakage, while adding auxiliary discriminants such as b‑tag scores, ΔR(top‑jets), and event‑level shape variables could further rescue low‑pT tops without inflating fake rates. Finally, benchmarking the refined cuts against a multivariate or shallow‑neural‑network classifier would reveal whether our handcrafted nonlinear ingredients are approaching the performance ceiling or if a data‑driven model can capture residual correlations that the current analytic form misses.
