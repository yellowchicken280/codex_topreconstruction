# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 669 Report

The modest gain to 0.616 ± 0.0152 shows that turning the raw BDT output into a log‑odds prior and replacing hard χ² cuts with Gaussian “probability” weights does indeed smooth the decision surface and suppress background more efficiently, but the penalty terms are still too aggressive for many borderline top candidates: the fixed Gaussian widths around the W and top masses, the simple correlation term linking the most‑W‑like dijet to the full‑top mass, and the un‑tuned logistic pT factor together over‑constrain the phase space and inadvertently discard a fraction of genuine signal, leaving us short of the 0.65 plateau. The next step should be a systematic hyper‑parameter scan—optimising the mass‑Gaussian σ’s (or replacing them with heavier‑tailed Student‑t kernels to better accommodate resolution tails), re‑weighting the correlation and pT components (potentially with a small learned scaling factor from cross‑validation), and exploring additional soft observables such as b‑tag probabilities or angular separations to enrich the likelihood product. A Bayesian optimisation loop or a gradient‑based meta‑learner could then fine‑tune the relative log‑space coefficients while preserving numerical stability, after which a thorough data‑driven validation will confirm whether the refined probabilistic envelope finally reaches and sustains the desired 0.65 efficiency without sacrificing background rejection.
