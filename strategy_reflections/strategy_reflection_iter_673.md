# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 673 Report

The modest gain to 0.616 ± 0.015 — still shy of the ≈0.64 break‑even point — suggests that the core idea of softening the high‑pT penalty and allowing any well‑reconstructed W‑pair to carry a candidate survived, but the implementation has not yet maximised the benefit: widening the top‑mass Gaussian with pT correctly reduced the over‑sharpened mass pull, yet the chosen scaling (linear vs. √pT) appears insufficiently aggressive for the extreme tail, while the geometric‑mean of three dijet Gaussians, though more permissive, also lets noisy sub‑jets dominate the score when one pair is poorly measured, diluting background rejection. The pT‑dependent asymmetry weight and low‑pT boost improve marginal cases but likely introduce a bias that lifts background fluctuations, and the multiplicative mixing with fixed exponents forces a rigid balance that may overly suppress complementary information from the raw BDT. Going forward, a systematic hyper‑parameter sweep (e.g. Bayesian optimisation) of the pT‑scaling law, the asymmetry exponent, and the low‑pT boost factor, combined with a more flexible aggregation (e.g. a weighted sum with learned coefficients or a shallow neural‑net combiner) should be tested; adding high‑level substructure features (N‑subjettiness, energy‑correlation ratios) and calibrating the logistic compression threshold on a dedicated validation set could further recover lost signal without compromising background control, ultimately pushing the efficiency past the 64 % ceiling.
