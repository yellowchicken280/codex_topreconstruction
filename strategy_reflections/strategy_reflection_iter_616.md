# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 616 Report

The high efficiency of strategy_v617 (0.616 ± 0.015) mainly stems from the two‑stage design that first culls blatantly inconsistent dijet masses, allowing the subsequent continuous discriminant to focus on genuinely top‑like candidates, while the pT‑dependent mass‑resolution σm(pT)=0.07·pT+5 GeV correctly enlarges the Gaussian width for low‑pT tops where detector smearing dominates; the dynamic exponent exp_factor = 0.8 + 0.4/(1 + pT/180) tempers the raw BDT score at high pT, preventing otherwise well‑measured tops from being over‑penalised, and the sigmoid pT‑weight introduces a smooth turn‑on around the historically hard 150 GeV cut, rescuing intermediate‑pT tops that would otherwise be lost; finally, using the geometric mean of the three Gaussian W‑mass weights gives a balanced, non‑biased measure of overall W‑boson compatibility, and multiplicatively combining these physics‑motivated pieces produces a discriminant that simultaneously respects resolution, BDT information and kinematic consistency, thereby raising acceptance without sacrificing background rejection.  The main weaknesses are that the chosen functional forms (linear σm, simple rational exponent, fixed sigmoid) and the modest hard cut (≈0.46) are still empirical, and the multiplicative form can be overly punitive if any single component is marginal; moreover, the raw BDT score may retain correlations with variables already encoded in the mass‑likelihood term.  Going forward, a systematic scan (or Bayesian optimisation) of σm coefficients, exponent shape, and sigmoid parameters on an independent validation set is needed, along with studies of threshold optimisation to map the full ROC curve; testing the stability of the discriminant under variations of pile‑up, jet‑energy scale, and b‑tag efficiency will gauge systematic robustness, and exploring a learned combination (e.g. a shallow neural network) or alternative aggregation of the three W‑mass weights could reveal whether the purely multiplicative architecture is truly optimal.  Finally, data‑driven calibration of the Gaussian W‑mass weights in control regions and the inclusion of additional topness or event‑shape variables should be pursued to squeeze any remaining gain in both efficiency and background rejection.
