# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 605 Report

The modest but statistically meaningful jump to an overall efficiency of 0.616 ± 0.015 (≈ 6 % absolute gain over the hard‑cut baseline) shows that softening the binary vetoes indeed rescued a sizeable fraction of genuine top candidates that were previously discarded by the rigid 3σ dijet‑mass cut and the fixed 1.2 exponent on the raw BDT; the heavy‑tailed Student‑t kernel and Cauchy‑like top‑mass weight kept events with moderate mass mismatches alive while still suppressing the most pathological configurations, and the pT‑sigmoid together with the softened BDT exponent restored low‑pT tops that had been over‑penalised. The gain, however, comes at the price of a slightly broadened background tail—because the smooth penalties are less aggressive than hard vetoes—so the optimal discriminant threshold must be re‑optimised, and systematic studies are needed to verify that the background‑rejection power remains adequate across the full kinematic range. The modular nature of the design now invites a targeted next step: perform a systematic hyper‑parameter scan (or Bayesian optimisation) of the Student‑t ν, the Cauchy scale, the asymmetry exponent, and the sigmoid steepness, validate each variant on independent control‑region data, and then calibrate the final continuous discriminant against sideband measurements to quantify any residual bias. If the background leakage proves tolerable, the refined smooth‑penalty scheme can replace the hard cuts in the production workflow; if not, a hybrid approach—keeping a loose hard veto on the most extreme outliers while preserving the smooth terms for the bulk—could capture the best of both worlds.
