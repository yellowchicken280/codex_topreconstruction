# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 760 Report

Strategy v761 moved the efficiency in the right direction because the physics‑motivated, smooth pulls on the top‑mass hypothesis and the closest dijet‑mass successfully rescued many borderline candidates that were brutally discarded by earlier, overly rigid cuts; the pₜ‑dependent σ in the Gaussian‑like top‑mass term correctly widened the acceptance for highly‑boosted jets, and the logistic pₜ weight amplified the discriminating power of the collimation variable where it matters most. However, the overall efficiency (≈61.6 % ± 1.5 %) still falls short of the 64 % target, indicating that the remaining components are still too punitive: the exponential pull on the W‑mass is likely too steep given the realistic detector resolution and final‑state radiation tails, the Lorentz–type collimation penalty over‑penalises legitimate moderate spreads (especially at intermediate pₜ), and the hard vetoes on extreme W‑ or top‑mass deviations still cut away genuine tops that the smooth terms were meant to keep. To push the performance higher, the next iteration should (i) broaden the W‑mass pull (e.g. increase its effective width or replace the exponential with a softer functional form), (ii) retune the pₜ‑dependent σ scaling (perhaps using a piecewise or power‑law model) so that high‑pₜ jets get a more appropriate tolerance, (iii) soften or replace the hard vetoes with continuous penalties, (iv) recalibrate the collimation factor—potentially by introducing pₜ‑bin‑specific exponents or by augmenting it with substructure observables such as N‑subjettiness or energy‑correlation functions—and (v) employ an automated hyper‑parameter optimisation (grid, Bayesian, or gradient‑based) to jointly optimise the exponents, thresholds, and logistic weight. A systematic validation across several pₜ regimes and against both MC and early data will then reveal whether these refinements can reliably lift the efficiency above the 64 % goal without sacrificing background rejection.
