# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 649 Report

The 0.616 ± 0.015 efficiency shows that the Gaussian‑likelihood treatment of the dijet and triplet masses, combined with the smooth pT weighting and softened BDT compression, succeeded in rescuing many signal candidates that were previously lost to hard rectangular cuts and an overly aggressive pT sigmoid; by allowing modest resolution smearing through a balanced Gaussian penalty and by keeping the BDT output sub‑linear, the method reduced the bias against medium‑pT tops while still suppressing the most egregious combinatorial backgrounds via the early >3σ veto—hence the net gain over the baseline.  Nonetheless, the multiplicative product assumes independence of the mass‑likelihood and BDT terms and can over‑penalise events that satisfy most but not all constraints, and the hard veto may still excise a tail of genuine tops that happen to wander beyond 3 σ in one dijet mass.  Going forward we should (i) replace the naïve product with a calibrated log‑likelihood or a second‑level classifier that learns optimal correlations among the Gaussian scores, (ii) re‑optimize the balance factor, Gaussian widths, and the exponent α using a grid or Bayesian optimisation on an independent validation set, (iii) validate the Gaussian mass parametrisation against data‑driven resolution functions to ensure the assumed σ’s are realistic, and (iv) explore adding complementary discriminants (e.g. angular variables, subjet‑b‑tagging scores) to further tighten background rejection without sacrificing the newly‑gained signal efficiency.
