# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 620 Report

The new strategy lifted the efficiency to 0.616 ± 0.0152 – a clear improvement over earlier cuts‑based versions because the pₜ‑dependent Gaussian widths gave a realistic smearing of the full‑top mass, the separate W‑mass width protected the sub‑system, and the spread‑based symmetry variables (m_spread and sym_factor) with smooth exponential and power‑law penalties successfully suppressed many combinatorial backgrounds without creating hard efficiency cliffs; however, the gain still fell short of the 64 % target because the logistic turn‑on on the raw BDT score and on the triplet pₜ, while elegant, under‑weights a sizable population of medium‑quality candidates, the fixed functional forms of asym_factor and sym_factor can be overly punitive for slightly asymmetric but genuine tops (especially at high pₜ where radiation skews the dijet masses), and the hard veto on large mass pulls removes a non‑negligible tail of real tops with modest mis‑measurements. To push past the barrier we should (i) re‑optimise the logistic thresholds (and potentially make them pₜ‑dependent), (ii) replace the handcrafted product with a learnable combination (e.g. a shallow BDT or neural network) that can adapt the weighting of spread, asym_factor and sym_factor across kinematic regimes, (iii) enrich the feature set with jet‑substructure observables (N‑subjettiness, energy‑correlation ratios) and per‑jet b‑tag discriminants, and (iv) calibrate the pₜ‑dependent Gaussian widths on dedicated control samples to better capture the true jet‑energy response. A systematic hyper‑parameter scan and cross‑validation on independent MC and data control regions will reveal whether the remaining inefficiency is due to over‑penalisation or intrinsic detector effects, guiding the next iteration of the algorithm.
