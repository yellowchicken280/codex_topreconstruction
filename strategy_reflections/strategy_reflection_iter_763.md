# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 763 Report

The gain in efficiency (≈ 61 % versus the previous ≈ 50 % range) stems from replacing brittle hard cuts with smooth, physics‑motivated probability factors that keep genuine tops alive while still penalising the most un‑top‑like configurations: the Gaussian mass terms faithfully encode detector resolutions, the tanh‑scaled BDT retains its raw discriminating power without letting a few extreme outliers dominate, and the exponential pₜ and spread terms replace binary vetoes with gentle down‑weighting that rescues moderate‑pₜ candidates and events with a single badly measured jet. By factorising the likelihood we also allow each observable to contribute independently, so the overall score reflects a joint probability rather than an arbitrary cut‑flow, which explains the observed increase in acceptance with only a modest rise in background rates. However, the current implementation assumes negligible correlations among the factors and fixes the functional forms (σ of the Gaussians, exponents, plateau scales) a priori, which could introduce hidden systematic biases or limit further gains; moreover, the smoother penalties may let in more background‑like events that were previously rejected outright. The next step is therefore to validate the model on independent MC and data control regions, perform a systematic scan of the width and exponent parameters (including profiling them against data‑driven calibrations), and explicitly incorporate observable correlations—either by introducing a modest joint term or by training a higher‑level multivariate (e.g. a calibrated BDT or neural‑network) on top of the factorised likelihood—to fine‑tune the trade‑off between signal efficiency and background rejection before deploying the method in the full analysis.
