# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 757 Report

Our modest gain to 61.6 % ± 1.5 % shows that the physics‑driven likelihoods and pT‑dependent resolutions do capture the improved mass resolution of boosted tops, and the geometric‑mean w_score successfully preserves events with a single outlier dijet, but the scheme still falls short of the 64 % target because the built‑in penalties for asymmetric radiation and the fixed 0.85 exponent on the raw BDT appear to over‑suppress genuine but imperfectly reconstructed tops, especially in the intermediate‑pT region where the σ_W model underestimates the true jet‑mass spread and the tanh‑based pt_boost saturates too early; moreover, the lack of any explicit b‑tag information means that the third dijet, which often carries the b‑jet, does not contribute positively to the score, limiting discrimination in cases where the b‑jet is well‑measured. Going forward, we should (i) re‑tune the σ_W(pT) parameterisation and explore a smoother, perhaps data‑driven, resolution model; (ii) replace the hard 2σ outlier cut with a softer, continuous penalty (e.g. a Student‑t likelihood) to avoid discarding asymmetric but signal‑like topologies; (iii) incorporate the b‑tag probability of the remaining jet into the overall top‑likelihood, perhaps as a multiplicative factor; (iv) revisit the exponent on the BDT output, testing values between 0.8 and 1.0 or even a learned non‑linear mapping; and (v) consider adding substructure observables (τ₃/τ₂, splitting scales) or moving to a shallow neural‑network that can learn optimal combinations of the existing likelihoods and kinematic variables. A systematic scan of these hyper‑parameters, validated on both simulated and control‑region data, should reveal whether the physics‑motivated enhancements can finally push the efficiency past the 64 % benchmark.
