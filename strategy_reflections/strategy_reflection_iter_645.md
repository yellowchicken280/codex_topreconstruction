# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 645 Report

The modest 61.6 % ± 1.5 % efficiency tells us that the new ingredients were indeed beneficial—most noticeably the adaptive top‑mass resolution stopped the pathological over‑sharpening at very high pT and the “best‑W‑match’’ kernel rescued a sizable fraction of events with a single well‑reconstructed W pair—yet the overall gain fell short of the 65 % target because the other components still over‑constrain the signal: the tanh‑shaped asymmetry penalty, while smoother than the previous hard veto, continues to penalise the fairly common moderate spread of the three dijet masses caused by jet‑energy‑scale fluctuations, and the logistic pT boost, designed to lift low‑pT candidates, is not strong enough to offset the loss of jets in the 30–130 GeV window, leaving a residual inefficiency in that region. Moreover, assigning only 45 % of the total weight to the physics‑driven kernels leaves the BDT’s learned discriminants dominating the decision, so any residual mis‑modeling in the BDT training can drown out the carefully shaped kernel contributions. The next step should therefore be a systematic hyper‑parameter sweep (e.g. vary the lower bound of the top‑mass Cauchy width, the exponential scale for the W‑mass term, the steepness of the tanh asymmetry function, and the logistic boost amplitude) combined with a re‑balancing of the kernel–BDT weighting (perhaps moving toward a 55 % kernel share) and the inclusion of ancillary observables such as b‑tag scores, angular separations, and refined jet‑energy‑resolution corrections. A targeted study of efficiency versus pT slices will reveal whether a piecewise or hierarchical classifier (separate low‑, medium‑, and high‑pT taggers) can capture the remaining lost tops, after which a modest retraining of the BDT on the updated kernel‑enhanced feature set should push the overall reconstruction efficiency past the desired 65 % barrier.
