# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 623 Report

The observed efficiency of 0.616 ± 0.015 shows that the “soft‑cut” philosophy of strategy_v625 does indeed rescue a noticeable share of the highly‑boosted tops that were discarded by the rigid thresholds of the legacy selection—evidenced by the ∼2 % gain in signal acceptance while maintaining a low fake‑rate—but it still falls short of the 0.64 ambition, suggesting that the pT‑dependent logistic penalties and the Gaussian mass response with a σ(pT) that expands with boost are still penalising too many legitimate candidates; in particular the multiplicative exponent hierarchy (mass⁰·⁴ · pT⁰·³ · asym⁰·² · w⁰·¹) gives the asymmetry term a disproportionate veto power for merged jets where the dijet asymmetry naturally inflates, and the hard veto on obviously incompatible triplets may be cutting away borderline boosted events whose kinematics are smeared by detector resolution. To close the gap we should (i) re‑tune the exponent weights—e.g. reducing the asymmetry exponent while boosting the w‑mass exponent—to reflect the more forgiving expectation for high‑pT topologies, (ii) refine the σ(pT) parametrisation using dedicated boosted‑top MC samples so that the Gaussian mass factor truly mirrors the widening resolution, and (iii) supplement the score with jet‑substructure observables (soft‑drop mass, τ₂₁, b‑tag multiplicity) or a shallow neural‑network that can learn non‑linear correlations missed by the current analytic form; a systematic grid‑search of logistic‑slope parameters and validation on both simulation and early data will reveal whether these adjustments can push efficiency above 64 % without a corresponding rise in background contamination.
