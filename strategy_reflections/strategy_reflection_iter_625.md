# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 625 Report

The 0.616 ± 0.015 efficiency of strategy_v650 shows that the multi‑stage veto plus the three new kinematic descriptors succeeded in rescuing many of the boosted‑top candidates that would otherwise have been lost to the rigid W‑mass windows of the classic top‑triplet tagger: Stage 1 rapidly removes obviously pathological triplets, freeing the BDT to focus on genuine three‑prong structure, while w_spread directly penalises the loss of resolution at high pT, mass_balance rewards the expected near‑unity relationship between the three‑body mass and the sum of the dijet masses, and asymmetry flags the highly asymmetric splittings characteristic of QCD background. By feeding these orthogonal observables through smooth non‑linear transforms (tanh, Gaussian, sigmoid) the final combined_score adapts continuously across boost regimes rather than imposing hard cuts, which explains the uplift in true‑top acceptance without a noticeable rise in fake rate. Nevertheless, the modest residual uncertainty hints at remaining shortcomings: the fixed functional forms may still under‑weight extreme‑boost configurations where jet merging collapses the dijet system, and the current weighting of the three descriptors could be over‑tuned to the specific simulation used, risking over‑training. The next logical step is to optimise the descriptor weights and transform parameters via a robust cross‑validation (including pile‑up variations), augment the feature set with complementary substructure variables such as N‑subjettiness ratios or energy‑correlation function moments, and ultimately replace the handcrafted product with a more flexible deep‑learning architecture (e.g. particle‑flow graph networks) that can learn the optimal combination directly from low‑level jet constituents. Parallel to these algorithmic upgrades, a data‑driven calibration of the combined_score—using tag‑and‑probe in semileptonic tt̄ events and control regions enriched in QCD multijets—will be essential to validate the performance, quantify systematic uncertainties, and ensure the method remains robust when deployed in physics analyses.
