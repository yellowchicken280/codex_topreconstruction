# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 674 Report

The modest gain to 0.616 ± 0.0152 shows that the physics‑driven mass‑pull likelihood and the dijet‑mass asymmetry term do succeed at taming the worst‐measured jet combos – the pull veto eliminates pathological configurations and the asymmetry penalty trims combinatorial background, which is evident from the cleaner BDT score distribution and the reduction of extreme outliers – but the hard three‑sigma cut and the very shallow non‑linear fusion also throw away a non‑trivial fraction of genuine top candidates, especially at high‑pₜ where the Gaussian width model may be under‑estimated and the saturating pₜ weight does not fully compensate for the lost resolution; consequently the overall efficiency still falls short of the 64 % break‑even goal.  To push beyond this ceiling the next iteration should soften the pull‑veto (e.g. replace the binary cut with a Gaussian‑penalty term or an adaptive sigma that follows the pₜ‑dependent resolution), refine the width parametrisation by calibrating pull distributions in fine pₜ and η bins or by learning a functional form directly from data, and replace the hand‑crafted weighted sum with a small multilayer perceptron or a boosted‑tree meta‑learner that can capture higher‑order interactions among the raw BDT, the likelihood, the pₜ weight, and the asymmetry factor.  Additionally, incorporating auxiliary information such as per‑jet b‑tag scores, jet‑energy‑correction uncertainties, and a dedicated training on events with known combinatorial ambiguities will help the model discriminate signal from background without over‑penalising good tops, and systematic cross‑validation will guide the optimal balance between background rejection and signal retention.
