# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 630 Report

The modest gain to a 61.6 % ± 1.5 % efficiency tells us that the physics‑motivated pieces of strategy_v651 are indeed pulling the tagger in the right direction—modeling the W‑boson and top‑mass fluctuations with Gaussian‑like likelihoods and using a smooth‑minimum “best‑W” choice keeps the loss differentiable and gives a clear boost to moderate‑PT tops where the previous soft‑Cauchy cut under‑performed, while the PT‑dependent logistic weight and low‑PT veto successfully prune many obvious fakes; however, the overall gain falls short of the 65 % target because the current parametrisation of those continuous factors is still too aggressive: the Gaussian widths are likely under‑estimating the full detector resolution, the logistic‑weight curve is either too steep or mis‑centered, and the veto threshold still excises a non‑negligible fraction of genuine tops with slightly off‑mass dijets (especially in the tail of the resolution). Going forward we should perform a systematic ablation study to quantify each term’s contribution, then fine‑tune the Gaussian σ’s (potentially using per‑jet resolution inputs), re‑optimise the logistic‑weight slope and offset (perhaps replacing the fixed logistic with a learned spline), and relax or adapt the veto based on PT‑dependent mass windows; simultaneously we can explore alternative smooth‑minimum formulations (e.g. a temperature‑controlled softmax) and consider a second‑stage, shallow BDT or neural‑network to recover any true tops lost by the veto. Finally, cross‑validation on mixed‑signal/background samples with realistic systematic variations will verify that any efficiency lift does not come at an unacceptable background‑acceptance cost, guiding us toward the needed ~65 % efficiency.
