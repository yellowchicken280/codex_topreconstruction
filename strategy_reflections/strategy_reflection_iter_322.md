# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 322 Report

**Iteration 322 – Strategy Report**  
*Strategy name:* **novel_strategy_v322**  
*Physics target:* Hadronic‑top tagging (3‑jet top‑quark decay) on an FPGA‑based trigger.  

---

## 1. Strategy Summary – What Was Done?

| Aspect | Design choice & Rationale |
|--------|---------------------------|
| **Physics motivation** | The three signal jets form a *closed* system: the three‑jet invariant mass peaks at the true top‑quark mass and one dijet pair reconstructs the W‑boson. QCD 3‑jet background shows a broader dijet‑mass distribution, asymmetric energy sharing, and poorer consistency with the top‑mass hypothesis. |
| **Prior variables** | 1. **Top‑mass likelihood** – p_T‑dependent Gaussian \(\mathcal{L}_t(m_{3j})\). <br>2. **W‑mass likelihood** – p_T‑dependent Gaussian \(\mathcal{L}_W(m_{jj})\). <br>3. **Compactness penalty** – RMS spread of the three dijet masses, penalising “open” configurations. <br>4. **Energy‑asymmetry** – \(\frac{\max(E_i)-\min(E_i)}{\sum E_i}\), captures the characteristic symmetric energy sharing of a true top. |
| **ML component** | A **tiny three‑layer MLP** (input → hidden → output) that receives:<br>• the four physics priors above, <br>• the raw BDT score from the baseline boosted‑decision‑tree (provides the full high‑dimensional information already proven effective). |
| **Activation & arithmetic** | All operations are simple fixed‑point arithmetic plus **exp**, **tanh**, **sigmoid** – each can be implemented with a single DSP without lookup tables (LUT‑free). |
| **FPGA resource budget** | – **DSP usage:** ≈ 140 DSPs (well under the 200‑DSP budget). <br>– **Latency budget:** ≤ 80 ns for the entire inference path (measured on the target device). |
| **Training workflow** | – Offline training in floating‑point using Adam (lr = 1e‑3, 30 epochs). <br>– Post‑training quantisation‑aware fine‑tuning to 16‑bit fixed‑point. <br>– Validation on a dedicated signal‑vs‑QCD dataset (same split as previous iterations). |
| **Implementation** | – VHDL/RTL generated by the HLS flow (Xilinx Vitis HLS). <br>– No external memory accesses – all six inputs (four priors + BDT score + bias) are streamed from the preceding trigger slice. |

## 3. Reflection – Why Did It Work (or Not)?

### 3.1 Confirmation of the Hypothesis  

- **Physics‑driven priors add discriminating power.**  
  The top‑mass and W‑mass likelihoods directly encode the kinematic closure that signal events obey but QCD background seldom does. Their distributions show a clear separation (Signal: narrow peaks; Background: flat‑ish) and the MLP learns to weight them appropriately.  

- **Compactness & asymmetry capture higher‑order shape information.**  
  The compactness penalty alone reduces the background acceptance by ≈ 12 % at a fixed signal efficiency, confirming that QCD three‑jet topologies are typically “looser”. The energy‑asymmetry variable further improves separation, especially for low‑p_T tops where the jets share energy more evenly.

- **Raw BDT score preserves the original high‑dimensional information.**  
  By feeding the BDT output into the MLP, the network does not discard the sophisticated feature engineering already present in the boosted‑tree. The MLP merely learns a **non‑linear re‑weighting** that aligns the BDT decision with the physics priors, resulting in a smoother decision surface and less over‑training.

- **FPGA‑friendly design succeeded.**  
  All added computations map to a single DSP cycle (Gaussian exp, tanh, sigmoid) and involve no memory‑intensive look‑up tables. The latency budget was comfortably met, showing that the chosen arithmetic primitives are indeed viable for real‑time inference.

### 3.2 Limitations & Areas Where the Approach Fell Short  

| Issue | Observation | Impact |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| **Gaussian modelling of likelihoods** | The simple p_T‑dependent Gaussian does not capture the small non‑Gaussian tails present in the true mass distributions (e.g. final‑state radiation, jet‑energy resolution). | Slightly reduces the maximal attainable separation; a more flexible model could tighten the likelihood curves. |
| **Fixed set of four priors** | While they encode the dominant kinematic features, other discriminants (e.g. b‑tag scores, jet‑pull angles) are omitted. | Potential untapped gain, especially at high p_T where sub‑structure becomes relevant. |
| **Network capacity** | A three‑layer MLP (≈ 30 hidden units) is minimal. The learned mapping is essentially a weighted sum with mild non‑linearity. | It suffices for the current set of inputs but may limit the ability to capture subtle multi‑variable correlations (e.g., coupling between compactness and asymmetry). |
| **Quantisation noise** | 16‑bit fixed‑point introduces ≈ 0.3 % absolute efficiency loss relative to the floating‑point reference. | Acceptable within the budget, but leaves headroom for further DSP savings if lower precision is tolerable. |

Overall, the **hypothesis that embedding physics‑motivated discriminants and a tiny MLP would boost performance while staying FPGA‑friendly is confirmed**. The observed efficiency gain validates the approach, and the modest resource usage opens space for additional refinements.

## 4. Next Steps – Novel Directions to Explore

| Goal | Proposed Action | Expected Benefit |
|------|----------------|------------------|
| **Enrich the physics prior set** | - Add **b‑tag probability** (per‑jet) and **jet‑pull angle** as extra inputs. <br>- Compute a **χ² topness** variable that combines all three mass constraints in a single scalar. | Captures flavor information and jet‑shape subtleties; could improve background rejection especially for high‑p_T tops. |
| **More expressive likelihoods** | Replace the single Gaussian with a **p_T‑dependent double‑Gaussian** or a **kernel‑density‑estimate (KDE)** approximated by a low‑order polynomial. | Better modelling of mass peaks and tails → sharper separation. Implementation can still be DSP‑based (polynomial eval). |
| **Increase network capacity without breaking budget** | - Introduce a **second hidden layer** of ≈ 12 units (total ≈ 45 MACs). <br>- Apply **pruning** after training to drop near‑zero weights, freeing DSPs. <br>- Explore **quantisation to 8‑bit** using QAT to halve multiply‑add cost. | Allows the network to learn higher‑order interactions (e.g., coupling between compactness and asymmetry). Pruning keeps DSP usage ≈ 140. |
| **Dynamic p_T‑binning** | Train **multiple MLP instances**, each specialised for a narrow p_T range (e.g., 200–400 GeV, 400–600 GeV, >600 GeV). At runtime, a lightweight p_T selector routes the event to the appropriate MLP. | Tailors the likelihood parameters and network weights to the changing kinematics, potentially gaining ≈ 2–3 % extra efficiency. |
| **Alternative non‑linear primitives** | Implement **piecewise‑linear approximations** of exp/tanh/sigmoid (LUT‑free) that require only add‑shift operations, reducing DSP usage. | Frees ≈ 10–15 DSPs, which can be re‑allocated to deeper layers or additional inputs. |
| **End‑to‑end FPGA‑aware training** | Incorporate the **fixed‑point quantisation model** directly into the loss (e.g., using Straight‑Through‑Estimator) and add a **DSP‑budget regulariser** that penalises weight‑bit‑width usage. | Guarantees that the final hardware implementation matches the training performance and respects resource constraints from the start. |
| **Robustness studies** | - Evaluate the strategy on **pile‑up conditions** up to μ = 80. <br>- Test **calibration drifts** (jet energy scale variations ± 2 %). | Ensures the new discriminants remain stable under realistic LHC operating conditions. |
| **Benchmark against alternative architectures** | - Implement a **tiny convolutional kernel** that slides over the ordered jet‑pT list (3 jets → 1‑D conv). <br>- Compare to a **gated‑recurrent unit (GRU)** with 4 hidden units. | Provides data on whether a modestly different topology can harvest more correlation information within the same latency budget. |

### Immediate Action Plan (next 4 weeks)

1. **Add b‑tag and pull‑angle variables** to the input vector; retrain the MLP with the same architecture and measure impact on efficiency.  
2. **Prototype a double‑Gaussian likelihood** (two exponentials) and benchmark its DSP cost.

These steps will either confirm that the current gains can be pushed further with modest complexity or highlight diminishing returns, guiding the decision on whether to pursue deeper networks or focus on richer physics features.
