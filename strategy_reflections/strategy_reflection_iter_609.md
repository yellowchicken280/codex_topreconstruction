# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 609 Report

The modest but statistically significant gain to 61.6 % efficiency shows that the physics‑driven, non‑linear melding of the raw BDT with explicit W‑ and top‑mass consistency terms succeeded in taming the combinatorial background while preserving a larger fraction of genuine tops than the earlier linear or fixed‑exponent combos—chiefly because the pT‑dependent resolution functions, the “soft two‑out‑of‑three’’ χ² penalty, and the tanh‑scaled BDT together kept low‑pT candidates from being over‑penalised and sharpened discrimination in the dense bulk of the score distribution; however, the result still falls short of the coveted 64 % barrier, suggesting that the exponential down‑weighting may still be too harsh (or insufficiently tuned) for marginally mis‑measured tops, that the sigmoid pT uplift might not be optimal for the full pT spectrum, and that the limited set of inputs does not fully exploit all available kinematic and tagging information. Going forward the most promising avenues are to refine the resolution modelling (e.g. calibrate the χ² scaling with data‑driven templates), explore alternative smooth penalty functions (power‑law or learned neural‑network penalties), broaden the feature set to include per‑jet b‑tag scores, angular correlations, and sub‑structure observables, and test more expressive architectures such as graph‑neural‑networks that can learn the jet‑triplet topology directly; simultaneous hyper‑parameter optimisation (including the sigmoid and tanh parameters) using Bayesian or evolutionary strategies, coupled with rigorous cross‑validation on independent simulated samples and control‑region data, will help determine whether these refinements can finally push the efficiency beyond the 64 % goal while keeping systematic uncertainties under control.
