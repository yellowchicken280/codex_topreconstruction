# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 634 Report

The modest but statistically significant rise to an overall top‑selection efficiency of 0.616 ± 0.015 — up from the ≈0.58 achieved with the hard pₜ cut — can be traced to three intertwined design choices: the smooth logistic promotion prevents the wholesale loss of genuinely low‑pₜ tops that the binary threshold was discarding, the Gaussian likelihoods on the two‑jet masses (together with the “single‑W‑candidate” filter) tighten the mass‑window acceptance without the combinatorial blow‑up that a simple cut would incur, and the fractional‑power weighting (t.score⁰·⁶ · L_top⁰·⁴ · …) lets the powerful BDT score keep its primacy while still rescuing events that are kinematically consistent but otherwise marginal. The optional soft veto (pₜ < 60 GeV) further softens the penalty on very soft objects, preserving efficiency for soft‑top signatures that were previously lost. The strategy does, however, expose a few weaknesses: the Gaussian widths and logistic slope are tuned on simulation and may be sensitive to mismodelled jet energy scale or resolution, the penalty on events with more than one W‑mass dijet can unintentionally suppress legitimate hadronic top‑pair topologies (e.g. in boosted t t̄ where overlap is common), and the added non‑linear ingredients increase the risk of over‑optimisation on the training sample, as hinted by the relatively tight statistical error but still unquantified systematic uncertainty. The next logical step is a systematic study of the logistic and Gaussian hyper‑parameters (including alternative “soft‑penalty” functions such as a tanh or a smoothed step) together with a re‑training of the BDT that ingests the W‑ and top‑mass likelihoods as explicit inputs, thereby letting the multivariate learn the optimal balance instead of hard‑coding fractional powers. Parallel validation on an independent MC set and on control regions in data (e.g. side‑bands of the dijet mass and low‑pₜ top‑enriched samples) will quantify any residual bias, while a complementary background‑rejection figure of merit (e.g. ROC AUC or background efficiency at a fixed signal efficiency) will confirm that the observed efficiency gain does not come at the expense of an unacceptable rise in false‑positive rate. If these studies vindicate the current configuration, the refined model can be rolled out with systematic uncertainties propagated to the final physics measurement; otherwise the insights will guide whether a more aggressive BDT‑only approach or a hybrid with explicit kinematic likelihoods is the more robust path forward.
