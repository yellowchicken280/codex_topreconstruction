# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 631 Report

The modest 61.6 % true‑top efficiency—well below both the 64 % plateau and the 65 % goal—suggests that the added physics‑motivated components, while conceptually sound, were overly aggressive in pruning signal candidates: the summed three‑mass Gaussian likelihood together with a strong Gaussian spread penalty and the multi‑stage vetoes appear to penalise legitimate resolution fluctuations more than background, and the tanh‑power mapping of the BDT score compresses the discriminating tail, reducing the effective separation. Moreover, the logistic PT weight, even after retuning, may still be centred in a region where top jets have a broad PT spectrum, causing unnecessary loss of midspectrum events. To move forward, we should (i) relax the mass‑spread penalty (e.g., use a softer chi‑square term or a mixture‑model likelihood) and systematically study each veto’s impact on signal efficiency; (ii) replace the fixed tanh‑power transform with a calibrated monotonic spline or a calibrated logistic that preserves the high‑score tail while still suppressing very low scores; (iii) re‑optimise the PT logistic centre and width on an enlarged training sample, possibly adding a PT‑dependent term in the BDT itself; and (iv) explore a unified multivariate tagger that ingests the three dijet masses, their pairwise differences, and PT as features, allowing the algorithm to learn the optimal combination rather than imposing hard analytic weights. By iterating these hyper‑parameter sweeps with cross‑validation and profiling the statistical uncertainty on the efficiency, we can aim to recover the lost signal and finally push the true‑top efficiency above the 64 % plateau.
