# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 665 Report

The 61.6 % ± 1.5 % signal efficiency we observed with strategy_BoostedMassBalance_v665 is a clear indication that the move from hard cut‑and‑count selections to a fully differentiable, physics‑driven weighting scheme does recover a sizable fraction of events that would otherwise sit at the edge of traditional cuts—especially the high‑pₜ (≳ 400 GeV) tops that are promoted by the tanh turn‑on and the Gaussian smearing of the triplet mass that compensates for jet‑energy resolution effects—while the W‑mass pull, variance‑based balance term, and the soft smallest‑deviation veto work together to keep the combinatorial background in check; however, the multiplicative nature of the score also makes the overall performance fragile: the variance penalty, if too aggressive, can penalise genuine signal configurations with modest mass spread (e.g. from extra radiation), and the exponentiation of the raw BDT score amplifies any residual mis‑calibration, potentially inflating background leakage at the high‑score tail. To push the efficiency higher (or to reach a more optimal signal‑vs‑background operating point) the next step should be a systematic hyper‑parameter scan—adjusting the tanh slope, the Gaussian σ, the strength of the variance term, and the exponent > 1—to quantify their individual impact on ROC curves; alongside this we should explore alternative combination strategies (e.g. a weighted sum or a learned meta‑classifier) that are less sensitive to a single under‑performing term, validate the approach on independent simulated and data‑driven background samples, and embed systematic variations (JES/JER, pile‑up) to ensure the observed gain is robust. Adding complementary substructure observables (soft‑drop mass, N‑subjettiness) or a shallow neural‑network layer on top of the BDT could further capture residual correlations and might allow us to tighten the soft veto without sacrificing the smoothness that gave the current improvement.
