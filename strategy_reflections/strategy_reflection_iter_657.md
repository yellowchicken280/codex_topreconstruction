# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 657 Report

The modest gain – an efficiency of 0.616 ± 0.015, still shy of the 0.65 target and even below the previous 0.638 baseline – tells us that the added physics‑motivated likelihood layers, while conceptually appealing, over‑constrained the signal topology and/or introduced sub‑optimal scaling; the exponential spread penalty on the three dijet masses, the χ²‑like down‑weighting, and the tanh‑capped raw BDT together compressed the discriminant range too aggressively, especially for genuine top candidates whose kinematics naturally drift from the ideal W‑mass clustering at higher pT, resulting in a higher false‑negative rate; moreover, the logistic pT‑weight, though intended to grant moderate‑boost jets leniency, may have been calibrated too sharply, penalising a sizable fraction of the phase space that actually carries signal. To move forward, a systematic re‑tuning of each component is essential: (1) re‑fit the pT‑dependent Gaussian top‑mass likelihood and the spread‑penalty width using high‑statistics MC to capture the true resolution tails; (2) replace the fixed tanh bound with a learnable squashing function (e.g., a calibrated sigmoid) that preserves dynamic range in the high‑purity regime; (3) experiment with softer χ²‑like weighting (e.g., a Student‑t kernel) to reduce harsh rejection of marginally inconsistent events; (4) perform a grid‑search or Bayesian optimisation of the logistic pT‑weight parameters to balance moderate‑boost flexibility against high‑pT purity; and (5) validate each iteration with cross‑validation and data‑driven control regions to guard against over‑fitting, potentially augmenting the model with a lightweight DNN that can learn residual correlations missed by the handcrafted likelihoods. By iteratively calibrating these pieces and quantifying their impact on both efficiency and background rejection, we can better harness the multi‑stage architecture and aim for the desired 0.65 reconstruction efficiency.
