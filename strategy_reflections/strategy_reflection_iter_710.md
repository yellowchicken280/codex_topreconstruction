# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 710 Report

The jump to a 0.616 ± 0.015 signal efficiency shows that the smoother, physics‑motivated likelihood weighting succeeded in rescuing genuine tops that were previously thrown away by hard 2σ/2.5σ cuts and an overly aggressive asymmetry exponent, because the Gaussian mass terms and the softened k = 2 asymmetry penalty let moderately smeared W‑mass dijets survive while still favouring the correct kinematic region; the tanh‑shaped p_T boost also helped to capture boosted topologies without inflating low‑p_T background as much as a linear boost would. However, the same softness inevitably loosened background rejection: the soft outlier veto (penalising only >2.5σ deviations) and the slightly higher BDT exponent (γ = 0.55) allow more QCD‑like three‑jet configurations to pass, so the purity of the tagger is likely lower than in the cut‑based version. The next step is therefore a focused re‑tuning of the Gaussian widths (σ_mjj, σ_top) and the asymmetry exponent to find a sweet spot between efficiency and fake‑rate, perhaps by making the outlier penalty a continuous function of the deviation rather than a binary step, and by calibrating the tanh boost on data‑driven sidebands to avoid over‑weighting high‑p_T background. A systematic hyper‑parameter scan (or Bayesian optimisation) on an independent validation set, coupled with a study of the tagger’s performance in different p_T regimes and under realistic pile‑up, will reveal whether a modest increase of k (e.g. k ≈ 3) or a modestly tighter outlier threshold restores background control without sacrificing the gain in efficiency. Finally, integrating these smooth likelihood terms into an end‑to‑end differentiable network (e.g. a graph‑based top tagger) could allow the model to learn optimal weightings directly from data, further improving both efficiency and purity.
