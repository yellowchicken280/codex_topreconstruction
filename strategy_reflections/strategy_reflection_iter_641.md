# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 641 Report

The modest 0.616 ± 0.015 efficiency shows that the smooth‑weighting idea did succeed in keeping the core of the signal alive—Gaussian kernels around the W and top masses and the pT‑sigmoid prevent the hard loss of events that binary cuts would incur—but the multiplicative architecture over‑penalised realistic top candidates: the chosen Gaussian widths were narrower than the true detector resolution, so even modest smearing caused a sizeable down‑weight, the exponential asymmetry term was too harsh for the natural dijet mass spread in genuine tops, and the final 3σ dijet‑mass veto clipped a non‑trivial tail of correctly reconstructed tops, especially at lower pT where resolution worsens. Moreover, raising the raw BDT score to a power > 1 sharpened discrimination but amplified the suppression from the other kernels, further eroding efficiency. To move toward the 65 % target we should revisit the kernel parametrisation—broaden the Gaussians, soften the asymmetry penalty (e.g. replace the exponential with a gentler power‑law or L1‑type term), and either relax the second‑stage veto to ≳ 4σ or make it conditional on the combined score. Switching from a purely multiplicative product to an additive or learned combination (e.g. a shallow neural net that ingests the individual weights) would also prevent any single term from killing the score. Finally, a systematic ROC scan on an independent validation set, with per‑jet resolution estimates feeding into the kernels, will allow a data‑driven retuning of the BDT exponent and overall threshold, preserving background rejection while recapturing the lost signal efficiency.
