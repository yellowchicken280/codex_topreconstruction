# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 708 Report

The new scoring scheme did introduce several physics‑motivated ingredients—pT‑dependent mass resolutions, heavy‑tailed Student‑t kernels, a harmonic‑mean W‑mass consistency term, an asymmetry penalty and a stronger BDT exponent—but the resulting signal efficiency of 0.616 ± 0.015 shows that the gains were outweighed by new sources of bias: the logarithmic σ(pT) growth likely over‑smoothed the likelihood at high pT, making genuine boosted tops indistinguishable from background; the harmonic‑mean penalty, while punishing a single mismatched dijet pair, proved too aggressive when resolution fluctuations pushed one of the three W candidates out of line, discarding otherwise good top hypotheses; the heavy‑tailed Student‑t (ν≈2) tolerated large deviations to the point that true mass peaks were diluted, and the chosen γ≈0.57 re‑compressed the BDT tail enough to blunt its discriminating power; finally the modest arctan(pT) boost could not fully compensate the resolution loss. To move past the 0.64 barrier we should recalibrate σ_top/W(pT) against the actual jet‑energy resolution per pT bin (perhaps using a linear or quadratic, rather than logarithmic, dependence), soften the W‑mass consistency by replacing the harmonic mean with a weighted RMS that tolerates a single outlier, experiment with a less heavy‑tailed kernel (e.g. Gaussian core with an exponential tail or a Student‑t with ν≈4), re‑optimize γ in the 0.50–0.55 range, and augment the score with complementary sub‑structure observables (τ₃₂, SoftDrop mass, subjet b‑tagging) that retain discriminating information at high boost; a focused grid‑search over these hyper‑parameters on a validation set should reveal a configuration that restores the lost efficiency while preserving background rejection.
