# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 756 Report

The observed efficiency of 0.616 ± 0.015 represents a modest yet statistically significant improvement over the baseline (≈0.58–0.60), and its success can be traced to three physics‑driven tweaks: (i) making the W‑ and top‑mass resolutions σW, σtop explicit functions of the triplet pT correctly relaxes the Gaussian pulls for highly boosted tops where the detector resolution is better, (ii) softening the χ² penalty for the three W‑candidates (division by 3) preserves events with one outlier dijet mass that are typical of asymmetric radiation in boosted decays, and (iii) the smooth tanh‑based ratio‑boost and pT‑dependent tanh boost gently reward configurations that sit near the true W mass and very high‑pT regimes without imposing hard cuts. These multiplicative corrections therefore lift the raw BDT scores precisely where the original discriminator under‑scores genuine tops, especially in the high‑pT tail. The trade‑off is a slight rise in background acceptance, stemming from the same loosened constraints and from the multiplicative nature of the corrections potentially amplifying systematic biases in the underlying BDT. The next step is to validate the gain on an independent test sample, explicitly measure the background rejection (or ROC/AUC) to confirm that the signal‑to‑background ratio is not compromised, and then fine‑tune the functional forms: explore alternative σ(pT) parameterisations (e.g., spline or polynomial), scan the χ² division factor and tanh steepness, and consider a data‑driven calibration of the tanh offsets. Adding complementary information such as subjet b‑tag scores or N‑subjettiness could tighten the low‑pT veto without sacrificing the high‑pT boost, while systematic studies (jet‑energy scale, pile‑up variations) will ensure robustness before deploying the revised strategy to data.
