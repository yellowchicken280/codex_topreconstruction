# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 725 Report

Strategy v735 proved that a more nuanced interplay between a BDT‑driven exponent and kinematic likelihoods can sharpen the top‑tag discrimination, as evidenced by the markedly tighter mass‑likelihood peaks and the reduced combinatorial background when the spread penalty and dynamic σ floors are applied. However, the hard first‑stage veto on dijet masses and the aggressive caps—σ_top = max(15 GeV, 0.01 p_T), σ_W = max(10 GeV, 0.005 p_T), the tanh(p_T/500) saturation, and the spread‑penalty term—appear to be over‑pruning genuine signal triplets, especially at moderate and high p_T where the veto discards events that a confident BDT score would otherwise rescue; this explains why the measured efficiency (0.616 ± 0.015) still falls short of the 0.64 goal despite the improved purity. The next step should be a systematic hyper‑parameter scan: relax the dijet‑mass veto threshold (or make it BDT‑conditional), loosen the σ floors slightly (e.g. 12 GeV and 8 GeV baselines), and tune the tanh scaling (perhaps p_T/400 or p_T/600) to preserve high‑p_T tops while still curbing overly generous likelihoods. Parallelly, replace the binary mass_factor = 1 – t.score with a continuous weighting (e.g. β·t.score + (1 – β)·L_kin) where β is optimized per p_T bin, and consider feeding the spread metric as an extra BDT input rather than a hard penalty. Finally, evaluate efficiency and background rejection in finer p_T slices to pinpoint where losses are greatest, then retrain the BDT on a dataset that includes these relaxed kinematic constraints. This targeted retuning should let the strategy retain its strong background suppression while nudging the overall efficiency above the 64 % target.
