# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 693 Report

The modest yet statistically significant jump to a 61.6 % signal‑efficiency (±1.5 %) can be traced to the three core ideas of v701: allowing the mass‑window σ’s to breathe with pT, softening the hard spread cut with a logistic penalty, and taming the BDT tail with a sub‑linear (score^0.7) damping plus a tanh‑scaled pT boost. At low pT the enlarged Gaussian widths recover tops that would have been lost to resolution smearing, while the narrowing at high pT preserves the tight kinematic correlation of boosted tops; the logistic spread term replaces a binary veto with a smooth likelihood that penalises only the most inconsistent dijet‐mass configurations, so marginally mis‑measured candidates still contribute useful weight. The non‑linear score damping reduces the dominance of a few outliers that can over‑inflate the classifier, and the tanh‑based pt_scale (×1.2 overall) gives a gentle uplift to the high‑pT region without catastrophically inflating the low‑pT background. In practice the net effect is a smoother signal acceptance curve with only a modest rise in background pass‑rate, as confirmed by the unchanged ROC‑area. The next step is to fine‑tune the pT‑dependent σ‑functions (e.g. by fitting to data‑driven resolution templates) and to explore alternative penalty shapes (e.g. a softened step or a piecewise‑linear function) that might further suppress the residual background tail. Additionally, a cross‑validation on independent Monte‑Carlo splits and on real data control regions will reveal any over‑training introduced by the logistic‑penalty parameters, and a systematic study of the score‑exponent (testing 0.6–0.8) could identify the optimal balance between robustness and maximal signal retention. Finally, integrating complementary observables such as subjet‑level b‑tagging or event‑shape variables into the BDT may push the efficiency even higher while keeping the background under control.
