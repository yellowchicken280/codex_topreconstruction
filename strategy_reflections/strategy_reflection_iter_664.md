# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 664 Report

The modest but clear gain to a 61.6 % signal efficiency (σ≈0.015) stems largely from the strategy’s shift away from rigid, cut‑based selections toward pT‑dependent, smoothly‑varying weights: scaling the Gaussian width linearly with the top‑candidate pT restores acceptance for highly‑boosted tops whose sub‑jet masses are smeared, while the tanh‑shaped pT weight gently ups the contribution of the region (pT > 400 GeV) where the tagger is intrinsically more powerful without imposing a hard threshold that would otherwise discard borderline events; the balance term efficiently culls combinatorial backgrounds that mimic two W‑mass pairs while leaving genuine overlapping top decays relatively untouched, and raising the raw BDT score to a power >1 sharpens the separation between signal‑like and background‑like candidates – a benefit that is preserved by the sigmoid replacement of a hard triplet‑mass cut, which avoids edge effects and retains a probabilistic interpretation. Together these ingredients produce a smoother, more inclusive ranking that can be thresholded to improve efficiency with only a modest impact on background rejection. Nonetheless, the approach may still be limited by the simplicity of the linear σ(pT) model (which could under‑ or over‑estimate smearing at extreme pT), by the fixed tanh steepness (potentially under‑weighting useful intermediate‑pT events), and by the power‑law amplification of the BDT score that can exaggerate statistical fluctuations or residual correlations with jet mass. The next step should be a systematic hyper‑parameter scan: test non‑linear or piecewise σ(pT) parameterisations, vary the tanh offset and slope, optimise the BDT exponent via cross‑validation, and explore alternative combination schemes (e.g. a shallow neural net or likelihood ratio) that can learn optimal weightings from data. Incorporating additional substructure variables (N‑subjettiness, energy‑correlation functions, b‑tag discriminants) and implementing a decorrelation technique (adversarial training or u‑Boost) to reduce residual mass‑score correlations will further tighten background rejection. Finally, validate the tuned tagger on independent Monte Carlo samples and, crucially, on early data (using control regions and data‑driven background estimates) to quantify systematic uncertainties (jet‑energy scale, pile‑up, modelling) before deploying the final selection.
