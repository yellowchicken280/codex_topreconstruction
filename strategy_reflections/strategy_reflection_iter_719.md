# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 719 Report

The shift from rigid χ² cuts to smooth, pₜ‑dependent Gaussian pulls, coupled with a continuous asymmetry penalty and a tanh‑scaled pₜ boost, clearly paid off: by softening hard thresholds the algorithm rescued a large fraction of genuinely high‑pₜ tops that would have been discarded, lifting the signal efficiency to ≈61 % (∼15 % above the static‑cut baseline) while still suppressing outliers; the geometric‑mean combination kept the three ingredients balanced, and the adaptive exponent on the original BDT amplified well‑balanced candidates as intended. Nonetheless, the equal‑weighting of the three terms may be sub‑optimal—mass‑pull information often carries more discriminating power than the dijet asymmetry—and the final multi‑stage veto, though lightweight, remains a hard cut that still culls a non‑trivial tail of good signal, especially in borderline regions of mass pull or asymmetry. The next step should be a systematic optimization of the weighting scheme (e.g. learn a small set of coefficients or use a shallow BDT to combine the pulls, asymmetry and BDT score), a pₜ‑dependent tuning of the pull widths and tanh scaling, and a softening of the veto into a penalty term rather than a binary reject; incorporating complementary sub‑structure variables (N‑subjettiness, energy‑correlation functions) and re‑evaluating the full ROC curve will help quantify the trade‑off and, ultimately, push the efficiency higher while preserving background rejection.
