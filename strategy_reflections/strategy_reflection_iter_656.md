# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 656 Report

The modest yet statistically significant gain to ε≈0.616±0.015 came from the cumulative effect of replacing hard, pT‑independent cuts with smooth, physics‑motivated functions: letting the Gaussian widths of the W‑mass likelihood broaden with the candidate’s pT respected the intrinsic degradation of mass resolution at high boost; the logistic turn‑on around 115 GeV eliminated the abrupt 130 GeV veto that previously trimmed away legitimate moderate‑boost tops; the spread‑factor rewarded the expected clustering of dijet masses around a common W value, reinforcing true three‑prong top signatures; blending the W‑ and top‑mass likelihoods in a pT‑aware fashion and squashing the raw BDT output with a tanh map preserved discriminating power while keeping scores bounded and preventing outlier domination; finally, a soft, pT‑scaled penalty on pathological dijet outliers trimmed only the most egregious background configurations without killing tails of real tops degraded by ISR/FSR or pile‑up. The continuous nature of these ingredients removed efficiency plateaus and recovered signal that was previously lost to overly stringent thresholds, albeit at the cost of a slightly softer background rejection and a modest correlation of the tagger score with jet pT that will need to be accounted for in analyses. The next steps should therefore focus on quantifying the trade‑off between efficiency and purity through full ROC studies, tightening the spread‑factor and penalty parameters via data‑driven calibration (e.g. using semi‑leptonic tt̄ control regions), probing robustness against pile‑up and generator variations, and exploring whether an additional regularization term or a small neural‑network refinement can further decorrelate the score from pT while preserving the gains from the physics‑driven smoothing introduced in v656.
