# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 648 Report

The modest gain of strategy _v650** over the baseline shows that the physics‑driven, non‑linear score does help retain genuine tops that are smeared by detector effects—especially the geometric‑mean Gaussian on the three dijet masses and the soft tanh‑scaled spread penalty, which together keep more candidates than a strict product of independent windows—yet the overall reconstruction efficiency of 0.616 ± 0.015 remains ∼2σ below the 0.65 goal, suggesting that the current weighting and penalisation scheme is still too aggressive for many true three‑jet top configurations. In particular, the sub‑linear BDT exponent (α≈0.6) may be compressing useful discrimination too much, the p_T sigmoid could be over‑discounting medium‑p_T tops, and the hard dijet‑mass window plus spread cut, while effective at purging the worst backgrounds, may be discarding a non‑negligible fraction of signal events that lie in the tails of the resolution. The next step should therefore be a systematic hyper‑parameter scan: relax the dijet‑mass window width, explore a milder tanh scaling or an alternative quadratic spread term, and test a slightly larger BDT exponent (e.g. α≈0.7–0.8) to restore dynamic range; simultaneously re‑tune the sigmoid’s steepness and offset to give low‑p_T tops a smoother survival probability. Adding richer inputs—such as per‑jet resolution estimates, refined b‑tag discriminants, and angular correlations beyond those currently used—could boost the raw BDT power, while trying more expressive multivariate models (e.g. XGBoost or a shallow neural net) may capture residual correlations missed by the present BDT. Finally, cross‑validating these adjustments on an independent simulated sample and quantifying their impact on both signal efficiency and background rejection will pinpoint the optimal balance needed to push the efficiency past the 65 % milestone.
