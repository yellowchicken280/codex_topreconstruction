# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 642 Report

The modest but statistically significant rise to 0.616 ± 0.015 in signal efficiency tells us that the core idea of softening the overly punitive mass‐window cuts paid off: by swapping the narrow Gaussian penalties for broad, Cauchy‑like kernels we granted reasonable smearing – especially at low pₜ where the detector resolution degrades – a gentle “cost” instead of a hard veto, and the dynamic exponent that relaxes the mass weighting when pₜ is small directly counter‑acted the worsening resolution; the sigmoid applied to the raw BDT output prevented the score from collapsing into a binary decision, while the soft pₜ gate kept high‑pₜ candidates advantageous without drowning the low‑pₜ tail, and the log‑space weighted geometric mean kept the four ingredients in balance so that no single term could dominate the product and kill efficiency.  Nevertheless, the gain is only ~10 % absolute over the previous scheme and the background rejection curve still shows a slight dip at intermediate pₜ, suggesting that the current kernel tails may still be too heavy or that the exponent‑pₜ functional form is not optimal; moreover, the weighting exponents were chosen heuristically rather than through a systematic optimisation, and we have not yet quantified the impact on the false‑positive rate.  The next step, therefore, is to place the kernel shape, sigmoid thresholds and exponent‑pₜ mapping under a formal hyper‑parameter scan (e.g. Bayesian optimisation) while simultaneously monitoring ROC AUC and stability across pₜ bins, to see whether a Student‑t kernel or a mixture of Cauchy and Gaussian components can better capture the true mass resolution; we should also explore adding complimentary discriminants such as b‑tag scores or event‑shape variables into the geometric mean, calibrate the raw BDT output to probabilities before the sigmoid, and validate the tuned model on an independent data‑driven control region to confirm that the improved efficiency does not come at the expense of hidden systematic biases.
