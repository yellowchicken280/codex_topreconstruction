# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 685 Report

Although the hard‑veto and Gaussian‑like quality factors sharpened the discrimination of the most W‑like dijet pair, they also proved too aggressive for real signal events: the 45 GeV mass window instantly discarded many legitimate top decays whose jets suffer modest energy smearing, and the broad exp(‑(Δ/20)²) down‑weighting of the remaining two pairs overly penalised configurations where only one of the three dijet masses is slightly off; together with the tanh‑penalty on the summed squared deviations, these ingredients removed a substantial fraction of true tops, negating any purity gain and leaving the efficiency at 0.616 ± 0.0152 – essentially unchanged from the previous plateau. The sigmoid‑bounded raw BDT contributed little additional discrimination because its output distribution was already compressed, and the steeper pT turn‑on may have introduced an unintended efficiency loss at lower‑pT tops. To break the plateau we should therefore soften the veto (e.g. expand the allowed deviation to ≈55 GeV or replace it with a smooth weighting), retune the Gaussian widths (perhaps Δ/6 for the best pair and Δ/15 for the others) to give the sub‑optimal pairs a more forgiving contribution, replace the tanh penalty with a calibrated χ² probability term, and recalibrate the logistic pT term to avoid cutting good low‑pT events. Moreover, moving from the sequential additive scheme to a joint multivariate classifier that ingests all three dijet masses (and angular variables) simultaneously—potentially augmented by a jet‑energy‑regression step—could capture correlations that our current handcrafted formula misses. A systematic hyper‑parameter scan with cross‑validation will reveal whether these refinements can lift the efficiency beyond the 64 % barrier or if the bottleneck is fundamentally limited by detector resolution and combinatorial background.
