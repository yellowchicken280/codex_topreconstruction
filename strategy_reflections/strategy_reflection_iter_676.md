# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 676 Report

The jump to an efficiency of 0.616 ± 0.015 under strategy_v676 shows that the three‑step un‑coupling of the BDT output from the mass‑penalties restored the dynamic range that was crushed by the product‑penalty in v675, and that the pₜ‑dependent Gaussian likelihoods coupled with a sigmoid‑scaled BDT delivered a more faithful representation of the underlying physics, especially for high‑pₜ top triples where the tighter σ windows rescued events that previously fell in an overly broad tail; the smooth asymmetry penalty and hard veto helped prune obviously mismatched dijet combinations without choking the signal, while the modest power‑law stretch amplified the high‑discriminant tail where signal‑background separation is strongest, preserving the rank ordering of candidates. Nevertheless, the gain comes at the cost of added complexity: the weighted‑sum coefficients and the exponent of the stretch are still loosely tuned, the background rejection (or overall significance) has not yet been quantified, and the pₜ‑dependent σ models may be over‑constrained in regions with limited statistics, potentially biasing the shape of the likelihood. The next steps therefore should include a systematic hyper‑parameter scan (including the weight fractions, the power‑law exponent, and the σ‑parameterisation) validated on independent control samples, a study of the ROC and significance improvement versus background rejection to ensure that the higher efficiency does not erode purity, and an exploration of whether the physics‑driven likelihoods can be fed back as additional features into a higher‑level BDT or a shallow neural‑net meta‑classifier to capture residual correlations; finally, side‑band data‑driven checks and a robustness test against variations in jet energy scale and b‑tagging efficiencies will be essential before committing to a production version (e.g., strategy_v677) that builds on the proven elements while tightening the calibration of the penalty weights and possibly replacing the simple power‑law stretch with a learned monotonic transform.
