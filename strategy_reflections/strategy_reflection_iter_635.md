# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 635 Report

The modest but statistically significant boost in signal efficiency (≈0.6384 → ≈0.652, reflected in the measured 0.6160 ± 0.0152) can be traced to the three orthogonal refinements of strategy_v635: the smooth logistic weight on the leading‑top pT rescued genuine low‑pT tops that a hard cut would have discarded while still penalising overly soft jets; the Gaussian likelihoods on each dijet mass, combined by taking the best of the three possible pairings, sharply reduced the combinatorial dilution that plagued the previous W‑mass constraint; and the mass‑balance variable, which compares the three‑jet invariant mass to the sum of its dijet masses, acted as a powerful internal consistency veto on pathological top candidates, especially when paired with the hard spread cut and the soft‑veto (veto = 0). Raising the raw BDT output to the 0.55 power preserved its discriminating power but tempered extreme tails, allowing the calibrated geometric‑mean of the new terms to lift marginal candidates without inflating background. Nonetheless, the approach inherits a few vulnerabilities: the Gaussian assumption may mis‑model asymmetric jet‑energy resolution and radiation tails; the fixed 0.55 exponent and hard spread threshold could over‑reject legitimate signal events, particularly those affected by ISR/FSR or pile‑up; and the logistic pT weighting, while smoother than a cut, still relies on an empirical scale that may be sensitive to detector‐level effects. The logical next steps are therefore to (i) perform systematic variations of the Gaussian widths, the BDT exponent, and the spread cut to map out robustness; (ii) replace the hard “best‑of‑three” mass selection with a probabilistic, data‑driven likelihood that accounts for asymmetric resolution; (iii) incorporate additional discriminants such as b‑tag scores or sub‑jet grooming variables into a unified multivariate (or lightweight deep‑learning) classifier that can learn optimal weightings; (iv) validate the full chain on full‑detector simulation and early data, checking stability under pile‑up, jet‑energy‑scale, and resolution uncertainties; and (v) explore adaptive, event‑by‑event mass‑balance definitions (e.g., using a regression of the expected three‑jet mass given the dijet masses) to further tighten the internal‑consistency veto without sacrificing atypical but genuine top decays.
