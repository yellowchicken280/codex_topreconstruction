# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 723 Report

The jump to a 0.616 ± 0.015 efficiency shows that the three‑pronged “physics‑first” overhaul succeeded in rescuing many genuine tops that were previously discarded: by letting σ_top and σ_W grow logarithmically with triplet p_T the Gaussian windows stay generous where the detector resolution worsens, while the soft‑max W‑mass weighting preserves the most W‑like pairing without throwing away the complementary mass information; the sigmoid asymmetry veto similarly rescues borderline asymmetric triplets that often arise in real decays, and the tanh‑based p_T saturation removes the over‑penalisation of highly‑boosted tops. The softened BDT mapping also prevented the machine‑learning score from overwhelming the well‑understood kinematic likelihoods, and the final multiplicative combination ensured that only candidates satisfying *all* criteria were strongly promoted. The remaining loss—reflected in the modest 6 % absolute gain over the baseline—likely stems from the rigid product of factors, which can still over‑suppress events that are strong in most but not all dimensions (e.g. a near‑perfect W‑mass pairing but a slightly asymmetric triplet). The next iteration should therefore focus on tempering this strictness: tune the logarithmic σ(p_T) slopes, shift the asymmetry sigmoid’s centre and steepness, and consider a weighted‑sum or learned gating (e.g. a shallow neural net) to combine the physics‑driven likelihoods with the BDT score rather than a pure product. Adding complementary substructure variables (N‑subjettiness, energy‑correlation functions, or b‑tag discriminants) and re‑optimising the tanh saturation scale (perhaps 300–500 GeV) will also help capture the residual signal, while a systematic cross‑validation on independent background samples will gauge robustness before a final deployment.
