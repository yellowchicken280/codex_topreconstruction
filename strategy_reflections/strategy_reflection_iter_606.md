# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 606 Report

The modest gain of the type‑B strategy shows that the physics‑motivated smooth modifiers helped preserve some low‑\(p_T\) top candidates but, because they were multiplied together, they effectively recreated a compound hard cut: any single component (the \(p_T\)‑dependent exponent, the Student‑\(t\) triplet‑mass kernel, or the Cauchy weight on the dijet mass) that slipped below its optimum value suppressed the overall discriminant and ate into the signal yield, leaving the efficiency at \(0.616\pm0.015\) rather than the targeted ≈ 0.65 while keeping background leakage acceptable. In particular, the Student‑\(t\) kernel’s heavy tails were perhaps too permissive, forcing a tighter \(w_{\min}>30\) GeV veto that inadvertently rejected genuine tops, and the Cauchy width around the \(W\)‑mass remained too narrow to accommodate realistic jet‑energy mis‑measurements. The next logical step is a systematic hyper‑parameter optimisation: scanning the functional form and strength of the \(p_T\) exponent, tuning the Student‑\(t\) degrees‑of‑freedom and scale, and broadening the Cauchy width, while also exploring alternative combination schemes (e.g. additive or log‑sum‑exp rather than pure multiplication) to avoid over‑penalising marginal events. Parallel studies using control regions should assess the impact of relaxing the \(w_{\min}\) cut on background rates, and re‑training the BDT with these smooth weights as explicit features could further harmonise the discriminant. A data‑driven cross‑validation on independent samples will then reveal whether the refined weighting can push the efficiency past the 64 % ceiling without compromising background suppression.
