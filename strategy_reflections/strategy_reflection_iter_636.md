# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 636 Report

Strategy v636 succeeded by directly confronting the three dominant loss mechanisms that plagued earlier versions: low‑pT tops, wrong dijet pairings, and jet‑energy mis‑measurements.  The smooth logistic pT weight resurrects genuine low‑pT top candidates that were previously over‑penalised, while a Gaussian likelihood for each dijet pair – with the maximal (most W‑like) value retained – sidesteps hard‑cut decisions and yields a cleaner W‑mass hypothesis.  The mass‑balance ratio (three‑jet mass normalised to the average dijet mass) exploits the characteristic ≈2.2 value of true tops, and the spread weight penalises large fluctuations among the three dijet masses, together suppressing combinatorial background.  A two‑stage veto (hard kinematic windows followed by a soft exponential penalty) removes extreme outliers early, keeping the final geometric‑mean product numerically stable; raising the product to the 1⁄7 power tames any single term’s dominance and keeps the score in a convenient dynamic range.  These physics‑motivated ingredients produced an efficiency of 0.616 ± 0.015, a clear improvement over the baseline, but residual inefficiencies remain – the soft‑jet rescue can admit spurious jets in high‑pile‑up, the Gaussian assumption may miss asymmetric mass tails, and the fixed exponent may still allow occasional term dominance.  Going forward, we should fine‑tune the logistic slope and turn‑over point, explore alternative (e.g. asymmetric or kernel‑density) dijet likelihoods, and consider learning an optimal combination of the individual scores with a multivariate classifier (BDT or neural network) trained on labelled top‑quark samples.  Parallel validation across pT and η bins, plus robustness checks against jet‑energy‑scale and pile‑up variations, will reveal whether the gains are broadly applicable or sample‑specific, guiding whether to iterate on the current hand‑crafted weighting scheme or to adopt a hybrid physics‑driven/ML approach for the next version.
