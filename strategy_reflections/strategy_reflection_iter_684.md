# Top Quark Reconstruction - Iteration 684 Report

The modest 61.6 % efficiency (well below the targeted 64 % plateau) suggests that the softened, additive architecture of strategy_v652 successfully made the tagger tolerant of isolated outliers but at the cost of blurring the very discriminants that separate genuine tops from background; in particular, the soft‑max weighting of the three W‑candidate pulls allowed a single well‑matched dijet to dominate even when the other two were poorly reconstructed, while the calibrated additive weights and logistic pT turn‑on were arguably too permissive, letting low‑pT, poorly measured candidates contribute non‑negligible scores and diluting the overall separation power. Moreover, the smooth shape penalty (logistic spread term) may have been insufficiently aggressive to suppress events with large dijet‑mass mismatches, resulting in a higher false‑positive rate that forced the operating point toward lower efficiency to maintain purity. To move past this plateau, the next iteration should re‑balance tolerance and discrimination: tighten the logistic pT turn‑on (steeper slope or a higher half‑point), re‑weight the W‑pull contributions (perhaps using a power‑law soft‑max or a capped max rather than a pure average) and make the spread penalty more data‑driven (e.g. fit the dijet‑mass‑ratio distribution and use a calibrated exponential or Gaussian‑tail suppression). A systematic hyper‑parameter scan or Bayesian optimization on a validation set, together with a study of ROC curves to quantify the trade‑off between efficiency and purity, will pinpoint the optimal weighting scheme; finally, augmenting the score with complementary observables (b‑tagging, angular separations, or pile‑up‑corrected jet substructure) could recover lost discriminating power while preserving the desired robustness to single‑jet fluctuations.
